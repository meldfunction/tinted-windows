Network effect
The more people use aliases, the harder they are to block.

Alias emails look suspicious today because they're rare. Some platforms have started rejecting common alias domains. This is the current state ‚Äî and it's also the counter-argument to itself.

As population-scale adoption grows, blocking alias emails becomes economically irrational. You can't block 30% of signups. The ratio inverts: platforms that reject alias emails exclude more users than they flag. Most privacy tools fight a rearguard action. Alias email adoption is the offensive play ‚Äî the more common it becomes, the less distinguishable it is, and the less reason anyone has to flag it.

What if there is no
MCP?

pane today is an MCP server suite ‚Äî it runs because your AI assistant calls it. But the privacy problem doesn't go away when the assistant isn't available. What does pane look like when it runs without a host, without a central server, without any persistent infrastructure that can be subpoenaed or shut down?

I
pane becomes a protocol, not a product

In the current architecture, pane is software you run that calls APIs. In the agent-centric future, pane is a set of signed instructions that any compliant agent can execute ‚Äî regardless of platform, regardless of who made the agent.

Think of it like HTTP: no single company owns HTTP, but every browser speaks it. A pane-compatible agent knows how to create an alias, provision a card, rotate an exit node, and report breach signals ‚Äî because those operations are specified in an open protocol that any agent implementation can follow.

The result: a pane-compatible agent running on Modal, on your Raspberry Pi, in a Cloudflare Worker, or inside a Tails session ‚Äî they all interoperate. No central server. No vendor lock-in. No single point that can be compelled to hand over your data.

II
The journalist workflow, agent-native

A reporter in an adversarial jurisdiction wants to file a story using a US-based publication platform. Their country blocks the platform. Their government monitors internet traffic at the ISP level. Their personal device has been inspected at the border.

Current world ‚Äî fragile
They install a VPN. The VPN provider has a legal presence accessible by local law. They create an account with their real email for the VPN. They file the story from their personal machine. Every step creates a record: VPN connection log, publication account, IP metadata at time of filing.
Agent-native world ‚Äî structurally different
They send a message to a pane agent endpoint via Signal ‚Äî a channel that's already on their phone and trusted. The agent (running on compute outside their jurisdiction) holds a clean alias identity for the journalist, provisioned with Monero, with its own VPN context and rotating exit nodes. The journalist dictates the story. The agent publishes it. No personal IP, no real email, no local device ever touches the filing workflow. The journalist's phone just sent a Signal message.

Speculative / 2027‚Äì2030
Breach intelligence without a central authority

In the current model, breach detection is centralised: HaveIBeenPwned, Firefox Monitor, your alias service's anomaly flags. These work because someone is watching all the traffic. But a centralised watcher is itself a surveillance surface.

The speculative alternative: a gossip protocol between pane agents. When your agent detects that alias notion-x7k@ has received mail from an unexpected domain, it publishes an anonymised breach signal to a shared reputation mesh ‚Äî not the alias itself, but a cryptographic commitment to a pattern. Other agents can verify the pattern without learning the alias. The mesh builds collective breach intelligence without any central party ever seeing your aliases.

This is conceptually similar to how Certificate Transparency logs work ‚Äî public, append-only, verifiable, but revealing only aggregate patterns, not individual identities. Nobody knows what you signed up for. Everybody knows which services sell data.

Design principles
I
Envelopes, not masks
Privacy isn't hiding who you are. It's not handing your home address to every acquaintance. You're still you ‚Äî just with a PO box.
II
One alias per relationship
Each service gets exactly one alias. When it's sold, you know who. When you're done, you cut that thread ‚Äî and only that thread.
III
Friction is the enemy
Privacy tools people don't use protect no one. pane removes all the friction. One command. You never touch a signup form again.
IV
Every alias is a canary
Unexpected mail isn't just spam ‚Äî it's a named, timestamped accusation. Your setup becomes a live, automated data broker detector.
