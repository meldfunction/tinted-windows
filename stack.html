What pane actually protects you from

pane is built to defend against data brokers, behavioral profiling, advertising networks, and traffic-analysis-affiliated tracking ‚Äî the commercial surveillance infrastructure that follows you across the web, links your identities, and sells your behavioral graph. This covers the vast majority of privacy threats that most people face in everyday digital life.

pane does not protect against nation-state adversaries, law enforcement with court orders, end-to-end traffic confirmation attacks, physical-world surveillance, or a compromised host machine. The Tor paper is explicit: low-latency systems cannot fully resist a global passive adversary who can observe both ends of a connection.

If your threat model is higher than this: Use Tails booted from a USB drive with no persistence ‚Äî or Whonix inside a virtual machine. For sensitive journalism, activism in a hostile jurisdiction, or whistleblowing: use a live OS image, don't touch your hard drive, wipe if necessary. pane can run inside these environments, but those environments are upstream of pane's scope. Know which tool you need.

Why tint levels?
Window tint is graded by VLT ‚Äî Visible Light Transmission. The lower the percentage, the less visible you are through the glass. Different transactions call for different opacity. A newsletter signup is 50% VLT. Your DMV appointment is 10%. The goal isn't maximum opacity everywhere ‚Äî it's appropriate opacity for each context.
The bootstrapping problem
You can't use Privacy.com without a real bank account. You can't buy Mullvad anonymously without Monero. You can't get a YubiKey without a delivery address. There is no perfect starting point. Start from where you are, add layers over time, and use Monero for new service payments once the initial layer is in place.
pane's scope boundary
pane addresses layers 3‚Äì6: identity aliasing, virtual cards, browser automation, fingerprint spoofing. Layers 0‚Äì2 are recommended but not integrated. pane cannot protect against a compromised host OS or end-to-end traffic confirmation by a global adversary (per Dingledine et al., Tor paper ¬ß3.1). The OS layer is upstream of everything pane does.
