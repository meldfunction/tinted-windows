ğŸ”´ Watching: Discord â€” February 2026

Discord rolled out global age verification requiring either a video selfie for facial age estimation or government ID upload through vendor k-ID or Persona. This is being framed as child safety. It is also a biometric data collection system at platform scale. In October 2025 â€” before the global rollout â€” a breach at Discord's previous vendor exposed government ID photos of 70,000 users. Discord responded by changing vendors and proceeding. Searches for "Discord alternatives" spiked 10,000% in 48 hours after announcement.

Current realistic playbook:

(1) Request a waiver before submitting. Many platforms that use Persona, Jumio, or Onfido have an alternative process â€” they just don't advertise it. Email their support and state explicitly: "I am not willing to submit biometric data or government ID to a third-party vendor. What alternative verification options are available?" Some platforms accept a manual review, a credit card match, or a phone call. The waiver exists because the legal exposure of requiring biometrics with no alternative is significant. You need to ask.

(2) TSA parallel: opt-out of body scan, request pat-down. This is the correct mental model. TSA full-body scanners are optional â€” you have a legal right to opt out and request a manual pat-down instead. The alternative is slower, more annoying, and still invasive â€” but it produces no biometric data. Apply the same frame to every KYC liveness check: there is almost always a slower, more friction-laden alternative that doesn't require you to hand a third-party vendor your face geometry. Ask for it explicitly. "I'm opting out of biometric verification. What is the manual alternative?"

(3) CCPA Â§1798.105 deletion request after every use. They collect biometrics as a condition of using someone else's platform, which does not exempt them from deletion rights. Fire a deletion request to Persona, Jumio, or Onfido within 30 days of every KYC interaction. Document date, platform, vendor. This is your evidence chain if a breach occurs.

(4) Build the paper trail. If you're compelled to use KYC and no alternative exists, document everything: screenshot the vendor disclosure, log the date and platform, save the confirmation. When these vendors are inevitably breached, documented interactions are the difference between standing in a class action and not.

The security vulnerability angleKey legal lever

EFF's July 2025 analysis made explicit what the litigation implies: "ALPR surveillance systems are inherently vulnerable to both technical exploitation and human manipulation. These vulnerabilities are not theoretical â€” they represent real pathways for bad actors to access vast databases containing millions of Americans' location data."

The specific legal theory for municipalities: Flock operates as a single point of failure. When a city contracts with Flock, it is creating a centralized database of its residents' movements â€” a database the city does not fully control, cannot fully audit, and whose security failures expose the city to liability. The Mountain View case is instructive: the city claims Flock turned on out-of-state access without their knowledge. If true, the city contracted for a surveillance system whose operator can modify access controls unilaterally.

The argument to your city council: "You do not fully control this system. Flock can modify who has access. You have already been shown to be in breach of state law as a result. The security vulnerability is in the contract structure itself, not just the cameras. You are liable for a vendor's decisions about your residents' data."
